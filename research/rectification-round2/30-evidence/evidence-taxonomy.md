# Evidence Taxonomy for Rectification Interviews

This page sorts evidence by how likely it is to help narrow candidate birth times without overfitting a biography after the fact.

## Use Order

```text
source quality
  -> event windows
  -> life-structure signals
  -> pairwise candidate comparisons
  -> appearance / presentation
  -> soft corroboration only
```

## Evidence Families

| Family | Why it may help | Typical use | Strength | Main risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dated life events | Best for timing coherence across multiple techniques | final narrowing | strong | dates may be fuzzy or remembered retrospectively |
| Life-structure themes | Splits charts by dominant life axis: career, relationship, family, health, mobility | candidate pruning | medium | broad enough to fit many charts |
| Role and vocation | Helps distinguish public role, status pressure, authority, service, and craft | candidate pruning | medium | people often describe their work in generic terms |
| Relationship patterning | Useful when a person has marked partnership, separation, caregiving, or attachment themes | candidate pruning | medium | overinterpreted from one relationship |
| Body / presentation / style | Can support Ascendant-family hypotheses | late-stage corroboration | weak to medium | cultural style, age, and stereotype contamination |
| Public biography | Good for known public figures or highly documented clients | support layer | medium | selection bias toward visible cases |
| Family structure and duties | Helpful when early responsibility, parent burden, or sibling dynamics dominate the life story | candidate pruning | medium | family narratives are often incomplete |
| Mood / temperament self-report | Only useful when tied to specific recurring patterns | support layer | weak | Barnum effects and self-attribution |
| Optional text / voice / image cues | Exploratory only | research layer | unknown | high risk of proxy leakage and overfitting |

## Strongest Signals

### 1. Dated life events
The practitioner consensus is still that timing methods work best when they can be checked against externally anchored events. This is the clearest evidence family for final narrowing.

### 2. Life-structure signals
These are the most promising non-event signals because they can split nearby chart candidates without asking the user to remember a specific day.

Useful subfamilies:
- career trajectory
- relationship structure
- family burden or duty
- health / crisis cadence
- mobility / relocation pattern
- visibility versus privacy

### 3. Candidate-chart contrasts
The best interview questions are often not "What are you like?" but "Which of these two life-shapes fits better?"

## Weakest Signals

### Appearance alone
Practitioners use it, but it should be treated as soft corroboration, not a core inference source.

### Generic personality labels
These are too easy to endorse vaguely and too hard to score cleanly.

### Free-form self-description
This tends to invite narrative fitting and confirmation bias unless it is heavily structured.

## How To Use This Taxonomy In The System

1. Use life events when you have them.
2. Use life-structure questions to reduce the candidate set before asking timing-sensitive follow-ups.
3. Use appearance only after a narrower chart family is already plausible.
4. Do not let any one signal family dominate the posterior without cross-checks.
5. Treat every signal as probabilistic, not categorical.
