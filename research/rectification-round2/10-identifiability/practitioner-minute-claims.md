# Practitioner Minute-Level Claims

## Question
When do astrologers claim minute-level rectification is possible, and how much of that claim is supported by measurement rather than workflow convention?

## Why This Matters
If minute-level precision is mostly a practitioner aspiration, then the system should optimize for calibrated narrowing and abstention instead of promising exact minutes on every chart.

## Findings
Astrodienst is unusually explicit about the sensitivity requirement for minute-based techniques. Its primary-directions page says the method uses roughly 4 minutes of birth time per year of life, and that it can only be used with birth times accurate to within a minute because a one-minute error would push predictions off by about a year. The related directions page says that when working with axes, a birth time needs to be accurate to within minutes or predictions become too vague.

That is a claim about method sensitivity, not proof of success. It tells us why rectification can matter at the minute scale, but not how often it actually succeeds on real people.

Practitioner workflows also treat rectification as a comparative process over a constrained candidate set. The Astrology Podcast transcript for rectification describes the common first step as narrowing a 24-hour frame to a smaller set of possible rising signs and then comparing charts against known life events. In that same discussion, the speakers warn that even AA-rated data can be rounded, and they recommend treating rectified times with caution unless the source is explicitly documented.

The time-of-birth pages on Astrodienst reinforce the same practical point: recorded times are often only approximate, and the Ascendant advances about one degree every four minutes. That makes the chart highly sensitive near the ascendant, MC, and house cusps, but only in the parts of the chart that depend on the time of day. Planetary longitudes themselves do not change enough over a few minutes to matter for rectification.

The measured side of the evidence is much weaker than the practitioner claim side. Controlled personality-matching studies like Carlson and Wyman/Vyse do not support strong chart-to-personality matching, and I did not find controlled evidence that validates exact-minute rectification from personality descriptions or appearance alone. That does not disprove rectification, but it does mean exact-minute claims are presently under-measured compared with practitioner confidence.

## Contradictions And Disagreements
The main disagreement is between practitioner confidence in minute-sensitive timing methods and the lack of robust external validation. Astrologers often say exact time matters greatly, while controlled tests of related chart-to-personality claims are mostly negative.

There is also disagreement about what counts as a trustworthy time. Astrodienst treats birth-record data as better than memory, while the podcast discussion stresses that even recorded times can be rounded or based on flawed clocks.

## Evidence Quality
- Overall quality: medium for method sensitivity, low for validation of success.
- Main limitations: practitioner sources describe workflow and sensitivity, not calibrated accuracy.
- What is based on consensus rather than measurement: the claim that rectification can usually narrow to an exact minute when enough technique convergence exists.

## Open Gaps
- How often minute-level precision is actually achieved on known-time charts.
- Which techniques contribute real discrimination versus post hoc narrative fit.
- Whether exact-minute claims hold only after a coarse narrowing step has already been done.

## Recommended Next Experiment
Create a blinded benchmark on known-time charts with three strata: exact birth-record times, rounded times, and family-memory times. Compare the success rate of minute-level rectification against a wider interval baseline and measure calibration, not just exact hits.
